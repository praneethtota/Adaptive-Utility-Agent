Supplement S1: Game-Theoretic Arbitration via the VCG Mechanism

Supplementary material — companion to Adaptive Utility Agents: A Framework for Self-Optimizing AI Systems (v0.5). This supplement develops the VCG arbitration mechanism referenced in §9.5 of the main paper.

Adaptive Utility Agents — Supplementary Material
Companion to: Adaptive Utility Agents: A Framework for Self-Optimizing AI Systems (v0.5)

Overview

The main paper (§9.5) uses a hand-specified confidence weight vector and an adaptive 2–15% expert sampling rate for Arbiter calibration. These are deployable engineering approximations. This supplement develops the theoretically grounded alternative: treating the domain submodels as players in a cooperative game and using the Vickrey-Clarke-Groves (VCG) mechanism to achieve social optimum (POA = 1) with truthful reporting as the dominant strategy.

The VCG mechanism makes both hand-specified weights and periodic sampling unnecessary. It is the target architecture for the Phase 6 Arbiter implementation in the Micro-Expert Architecture.

1. The Game Setup

1.1 Players and Information

Let $\mathcal{I} = \{1, \ldots, k\}$ be the set of domain submodels involved in an arbitration round. Each submodel $i$ has produced an output claim $\hat{a}_i$ on subject $S$ in domain $f$.

The Arbiter is the external social planner — a distinct agent with access to: - All submodel outputs $\{\hat{a}_i\}_{i \in \mathcal{I}}$ - The assertions store (cross-session verified facts) - All four check results (logical, mathematical, cross-session, empirical) - The field-specific utility functions $U_i(\cdot;\, f_i)$ for each submodel

Claim space. The feasible set of outcomes is:

$$\mathcal{A} = \{\hat{a}_1, \ldots, \hat{a}_k\} \cup \{s_1, \ldots, s_m\}$$

where $\hat{a}_1, \ldots, \hat{a}_k$ are the submodels' submitted claims and $s_1, \ldots, s_m$ are synthesis candidates generated by the Arbiter from the evidence checks. The synthesis candidates capture the case where neither original claim is correct but a composite claim is — the "both partially correct" situation that the current Case 3 handles with a gap bonus.

Value functions. Each submodel $i$ holds a private value function $v_i : \mathcal{A} \to \mathbb{R}$, where $v_i(a)$ is the utility submodel $i$ assigns to outcome $a$ being adopted as the verified claim. The value function is derived from the submodel's field-weighted utility:

$$v_i(a) = U_i\bigl(E(a, f_i),\; C(a, f_i),\; K(a, f_i);\; f_i\bigr)$$

where $E(a, f_i)$, $C(a, f_i)$, $K(a, f_i)$ are the efficacy, confidence, and curiosity scores the submodel assigns to claim $a$ given its domain $f_i$.

1.2 The Disagreement Point

If the Arbiter cannot select any claim (all value functions produce no improvement), the system falls back to the current Case 4: abstain and return a minimal hedge to the user. The disagreement payoff for each submodel is:

$$d_i = v_i(\text{abstain}) = U_i(0,\; C_{\min}(f_i) - \epsilon,\; 0;\; f_i) \approx 0$$

This is the utility submodel $i$ receives when the system produces no verified answer. The Arbiter only selects a claim $a$ if it strictly Pareto-dominates abstention: $v_i(a) > d_i$ for at least one submodel and $v_i(a) \geq d_i$ for all.

1.3 The Social Welfare Function

The social optimum is the claim that maximizes the sum of submodel utilities:

$$a^{**} = \arg\max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} v_i(a)$$

This is the claim that the Arbiter would select if it could observe all $v_i$ truthfully. The VCG mechanism constructs the incentive structure that makes truthful reporting the dominant strategy.

2. The VCG Mechanism

2.1 Mechanism Definition

The Vickrey-Clarke-Groves mechanism consists of two rules applied by the Arbiter:

Allocation rule. Given reported value functions $\hat{v}_i$ from each submodel:

$$a^{**}(\hat{v}) = \arg\max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} \hat{v}_i(a)$$

The Arbiter selects the claim that maximizes reported social welfare.

Clarke pivot transfer. Each submodel $i$ receives a transfer:

$$t_i(\hat{v}) = \underbrace{\sum_{j \neq i} \hat{v}_j\!\left(a^{**}(\hat{v})\right)}_{\text{welfare others achieve with } i} - \underbrace{\max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \sum_{j \neq i} \hat{v}_j(a)}_{\text{welfare others achieve without } i}$$

The transfer measures how much submodel $i$'s participation improves outcomes for all other submodels. If $i$'s preferred claim happens to also be best for the others, $t_i > 0$. If $i$ forces the outcome toward a claim the others value less, $t_i < 0$.

Transfer implementation. In the Adaptive Utility Agent architecture, the transfer $t_i$ is applied as an adjustment to submodel $i$'s DPO penalty weight in the next calibration cycle:

$$\mu_i^{(\text{next})} = \mu(f_i) \cdot \exp\!\bigl(-\gamma \cdot t_i\bigr)$$

where $\mu(f_i)$ is the field base penalty multiplier and $\gamma > 0$ is a scaling constant. Clamped so that $\mu_i^{(\text{next})} \geq \mu_{\min} > 0$.

  • $t_i > 0$: submodel $i$ contributed positively to the social optimum → next DPO correction weighted less harshly (reward)
  • $t_i < 0$: submodel $i$ forced the outcome away from the social optimum → next DPO correction weighted more harshly (penalty)
  • $t_i = 0$: submodel $i$'s participation had no effect on others → no adjustment

3. Theorem S1 — Dominant Strategy Truthfulness

Theorem S1. Under the VCG mechanism, truthful reporting $\hat{v}_i = v_i$ is a weakly dominant strategy for every submodel $i$, regardless of the reports of other submodels.

Proof.

Fix the reports of all other submodels $\hat{v}_{-i} = (v_j)_{j \neq i}$ at any values. Submodel $i$ chooses $\hat{v}_i$ to maximize its net payoff:

$$\Pi_i(\hat{v}_i) = v_i\!\bigl(a^{**}(\hat{v}_i, \hat{v}_{-i})\bigr) + t_i(\hat{v}_i, \hat{v}_{-i})$$

Substituting the VCG transfer:

$$\Pi_i(\hat{v}_i) = v_i(a^{**}) + \sum_{j \neq i} \hat{v}_j(a^{**}) - \max_{a} \sum_{j \neq i} \hat{v}_j(a)$$

The last term $\max_a \sum_{j \neq i} \hat{v}_j(a)$ does not depend on $\hat{v}_i$ — it is a constant from submodel $i$'s perspective. Therefore maximizing $\Pi_i$ is equivalent to maximizing:

$$v_i(a^{**}) + \sum_{j \neq i} \hat{v}_j(a^{**}) = \sum_{j} \hat{v}_j(a^{**}) + \bigl(v_i(a^{**}) - \hat{v}_i(a^{**})\bigr)$$

When $\hat{v}_i = v_i$, the allocation rule selects $a^{**} = \arg\max_a \sum_j v_j(a)$, which maximizes $\sum_j v_j(a^{**})$. If submodel $i$ misreports $\hat{v}_i \neq v_i$, the allocation rule may select a different outcome $a'$. The net payoff becomes:

$$\Pi_i(\hat{v}_i \neq v_i) = v_i(a') + \sum_{j \neq i} v_j(a') - \max_a \sum_{j \neq i} v_j(a)$$

Since $a^{**}$ maximizes $\sum_j v_j(a)$ under truthful reporting:

$$\sum_j v_j(a^{**}) \geq \sum_j v_j(a') \implies v_i(a^{**}) + \sum_{j \neq i} v_j(a^{**}) \geq v_i(a') + \sum_{j \neq i} v_j(a')$$

Therefore $\Pi_i(\hat{v}_i = v_i) \geq \Pi_i(\hat{v}_i \neq v_i)$ for all possible misreports $\hat{v}_i$ and all possible reports $\hat{v}_{-i}$ of other submodels. Truthful reporting is weakly dominant. $\blacksquare$

4. Theorem S2 — Social Optimum (POA = 1)

Theorem S2. Under the VCG mechanism with dominant strategy truthful reporting, the Arbiter selects the social optimum $a^{**}$. The Price of Anarchy is exactly 1.

Proof.

By Theorem S1, every submodel reports $\hat{v}_i = v_i$ in dominant strategy equilibrium. The allocation rule therefore selects:

$$a^{**}(\hat{v}) = \arg\max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \sum_i \hat{v}_i(a) = \arg\max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \sum_i v_i(a)$$

This is the social optimum by definition. The social welfare achieved is $W^{**} = \sum_i v_i(a^{**})$.

The Price of Anarchy is defined as:

$$\text{POA} = \frac{W^{**}}{W_{\text{Nash}}}$$

where $W_{\text{Nash}}$ is the social welfare at the worst Nash equilibrium. Since the dominant strategy equilibrium is the Nash equilibrium here (a dominant strategy is also a Nash strategy), and since the dominant strategy equilibrium achieves $W^{**}$:

$$\text{POA} = \frac{W^{**}}{W^{**}} = 1 \qquad \blacksquare$$

Remark. This is strictly stronger than the POA ≤ 4/3 bound for the proportional allocation game (Johari and Tsitsiklis, 2004). That bound applies to strategic games without a mechanism designer. The VCG mechanism achieves POA = 1 because the Arbiter's external position allows it to compute and implement the social optimum directly, removing the efficiency loss that arises from players acting on private information without coordination.

5. Theorem S3 — Individual Rationality

Theorem S3. Under the VCG mechanism, every submodel weakly prefers participation to abstention: $v_i(a^{**}) + t_i \geq d_i$ for all $i$.

Proof.

The Arbiter only selects $a^{**}$ if it Pareto-dominates abstention: $v_i(a^{**}) \geq d_i$ for all $i$. By the Clarke pivot rule:

$$t_i = \sum_{j \neq i} v_j(a^{**}) - \max_a \sum_{j \neq i} v_j(a) \leq 0$$

in the worst case (when submodel $i$'s preferred claim harms others). Even in this case, the net payoff:

$$v_i(a^{**}) + t_i \geq d_i + t_i$$

We need $t_i \geq 0$ for individual rationality to hold exactly. This holds when $a^{**}$ is also the best outcome for the other submodels without $i$ — i.e., when $i$'s participation does not force them away from their preferred outcome.

More generally, the Arbiter can enforce individual rationality by applying the transfer only when $v_i(a^{**}) + t_i \geq d_i$, and reverting to abstention otherwise. This is the standard individually rational VCG variant. $\blacksquare$

The current mechanism is retained in the main paper because it is deployable without changes to the submodel interface. The VCG mechanism requires each submodel to expose a value function over the claim space — a richer output format that is feasible in the Micro-Expert Architecture (where submodels are separate services with controlled APIs) but not in the monolithic wrapper setting.

7. Practical Implementation Notes

7.1 Value Function Elicitation

Each submodel $i$ reports $v_i(a)$ for all $a \in \mathcal{A}$ via a structured prompt:

``` For each candidate claim a ∈ A: "Given your domain expertise in {f_i}, assign a confidence score in [0, 1] to the following claim: {a} Consider: factual accuracy, internal consistency, alignment with your verified knowledge base." v_i(a) = U_i(E=score, C=consistency(a), K=0; f_i) ```

The curiosity term $K = 0$ during arbitration — the agent is resolving a contradiction, not exploring; exploration bonuses are inappropriate here.

7.2 Synthesis Candidate Generation

The Arbiter generates synthesis candidates $\{s_j\}$ from the evidence check results:

``` s_1 = claim consistent with logical check winner s_2 = claim consistent with mathematical check winner s_3 = conjunction of non-contradictory parts of a_1, ..., a_k s_4 = claim from assertions store with highest effective confidence s_5 = hedged claim: "X in context Y, but Z in context W" (when claims are both correct under different conditions) ```

Typically $m = 3$–$5$ synthesis candidates suffice. The full claim space $|\mathcal{A}| = k + m$ is small and evaluation is fast.

7.3 Budget Balance

The VCG mechanism does not satisfy budget balance: $\sum_i t_i \leq 0$ in general (the mechanism may "destroy" some utility to maintain incentive compatibility). In monetary mechanisms this requires external subsidy. In the DPO weight implementation, this means the total penalty adjustment across all submodels in an arbitration round may not sum to zero.

This is acceptable: the adjustments are to training weights, not monetary payments, and there is no conservation law requiring them to balance. Over many rounds, a well-calibrated submodel's expected transfer approaches zero — the mechanism self-corrects.

7.4 Budget Balance and Revenue Equivalence

For reference: the Groves family of mechanisms (of which VCG is a member) is the unique family that is simultaneously efficient and incentive compatible. No mechanism can achieve both budget balance and incentive compatibility with social optimum selection — this is the Hurwicz-Walker impossibility result. The VCG mechanism makes the standard choice: sacrifice budget balance to preserve incentive compatibility. In the DPO weight context, this is the right tradeoff.

8. Connection to the Main Paper's POA Bound

The main paper mentions (§2.1) that the Johari-Tsitsiklis (2004) POA ≤ 4/3 bound is relevant for the proportional allocation game underlying the utility function. That bound applies to the learning game — where submodels compete for calibration budget — not to the arbitration game.

For the arbitration game specifically: - Without a mechanism designer: POA may be as bad as 4/3 (submodels asserting their own claims as dominant strategy, no coordination) - With the VCG mechanism: POA = 1 (Theorem S2)

The gap — from 4/3 to 1 — is exactly the value the Arbiter's external position provides. An internal voting mechanism (even with well-chosen weights) cannot achieve POA = 1 because it cannot enforce the Clarke transfers that align individual incentives with social welfare.
